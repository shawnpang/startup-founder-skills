# Landing Page

## When to Use
- Creating a new landing page from scratch (product launch, feature page, waitlist).
- Auditing an existing page for conversion rate optimization (CRO).
- Rewriting headlines, CTAs, or hero sections.
- Structuring page sections for a specific audience segment.
- A/B test variant ideation for copy or layout changes.

## Context Required
- **From startup-context**: product description, ICP (ideal customer profile), value proposition, competitive positioning, stage, tone of voice.
- **From the user**: page URL or current copy (if auditing), goal of the page (signup, demo request, purchase), traffic source (ads, organic, referral), any existing conversion data.

## Workflow
1. **Clarify the page goal** — Identify the single primary conversion action. Every page gets one job.
2. **Audit or draft the hero section** — Apply the Value Proposition Canvas: what pain does the visitor arrive with, what outcome do they want, why is this product the bridge?
3. **Write the headline stack** — Draft 3-5 headline variants using these formulas:
   - **Outcome-driven**: "Get [desired outcome] without [pain point]"
   - **Specificity**: Include a number, timeframe, or concrete result
   - **Curiosity gap**: Hint at a mechanism without revealing it fully
   - **Social proof embedded**: "Join [N] teams who [achieve outcome]"
   - **Direct address**: "You [do painful thing]. There's a better way."
4. **Structure page sections** — Follow the high-converting section order:
   - Hero (headline + subhead + CTA + social proof nugget)
   - Problem agitation (name the pain explicitly)
   - Solution overview (how it works in 3 steps)
   - Feature-benefit blocks (features framed as outcomes)
   - Social proof section (testimonials, logos, stats)
   - Objection handling (FAQ or comparison table)
   - Final CTA with urgency or risk reversal
5. **Apply copy framework** — Choose one based on awareness level:
   - **PAS** (Problem-Agitate-Solve): best for problem-aware audiences
   - **AIDA** (Attention-Interest-Desire-Action): best for mixed-awareness traffic
   - **BAB** (Before-After-Bridge): best for solution-aware audiences
6. **Evaluate trust signals** — Check for: customer logos, testimonial quotes with names/photos, security badges, guarantee language, press mentions, specific metrics.
7. **CRO checklist** — Run through: CTA above the fold, contrast color on CTA button, single primary action per screen, mobile responsiveness, page load speed, no navigation links competing with CTA.

## Output Format
- **If auditing**: A scored breakdown (1-10) across 6 dimensions (headline clarity, value prop strength, CTA effectiveness, visual hierarchy, trust signals, mobile experience) with specific rewrite suggestions.
- **If drafting**: Full page copy organized by section, with 2-3 headline variants and CTA text options. Include brief notes on layout/visual recommendations.

## Frameworks & Best Practices
- **One page, one goal**: Never give the visitor two equal choices. Every element should push toward the primary CTA.
- **Headline carries 80% of the weight**: If the headline doesn't stop the scroll, nothing else matters. Test headlines before anything else.
- **Specificity beats cleverness**: "Save 12 hours per week on reporting" outperforms "Work smarter, not harder" every time.
- **Social proof placement**: Place a small proof element (e.g., "Trusted by 500+ teams") within the hero section, and a dedicated proof section below the fold.
- **Above-the-fold rule**: The visitor should understand what you do, who it's for, and what to do next within 5 seconds of landing.
- **Button copy**: Use first-person, action-oriented CTA text ("Start my free trial") rather than generic ("Submit", "Learn more").
- **Risk reversal**: Always address "what if it doesn't work for me?" near the final CTA — free trial, money-back guarantee, or no-commitment language.
- **Visual hierarchy**: Use size, color, and whitespace to create a clear reading path. The eye should flow: headline, subhead, CTA, supporting content.
- **Mobile-first**: Over 60% of traffic is mobile. Design for thumb zones and ensure CTAs are tappable without zooming.
- **Awareness-level matching**: Match copy depth to visitor awareness. Unaware visitors need more problem education before the solution. Most-aware visitors want pricing and a CTA immediately. Traffic source reveals awareness level — paid search is typically solution-aware, social ads are typically problem-aware or unaware.
- **Subheadline does the heavy lifting**: The headline earns attention, but the subheadline explains the value. Use it to clarify who the product is for, what it does, and why it matters. Do not waste it on vague taglines.
- **Testimonial specificity**: "This product is great" is worthless. "We reduced onboarding time from 3 weeks to 2 days" is persuasive. Coach customers to provide specific, outcome-driven testimonials with numbers.
- **Reduce cognitive load**: Every additional choice, link, or piece of information on the page adds friction. Ruthlessly cut anything that does not directly support the primary conversion action.

## Examples

**Example 1: Audit request**
> "Here's our landing page copy. We're getting 15k visitors/month but only 1.2% conversion. What's wrong?"

Good output: A 6-dimension CRO scorecard with specific issues identified (e.g., "Headline is feature-focused rather than outcome-focused — rewrite from 'AI-Powered Analytics Platform' to 'Cut your reporting time from 4 hours to 15 minutes'"). Includes priority-ranked list of 5 changes with expected impact.

**Example 2: New page draft**
> "We're launching a waitlist page for our developer tool. Target audience is backend engineers frustrated with deployment complexity."

Good output: Full section-by-section copy using PAS framework. Hero headline: "Deploy to production in 3 commands, not 30 steps." Subhead addressing the pain. 3-step "how it works" section. Social proof placeholder guidance. CTA: "Join 2,400 engineers on the waitlist." Includes 2 alternative headline options and notes on visual layout.

**Example 3: A/B test variants**
> "Our signup page converts at 3.5%. We want to A/B test improvements. What should we test first?"

Good output: Recommends testing in priority order: (1) headline — 3 variants reframing the value prop from feature-first to outcome-first, (2) CTA button copy — test "Start free trial" vs. "See it in action" vs. "Get started in 2 minutes", (3) adding a trust element to the hero — customer count or logo bar. Each test includes the control, variant, hypothesis, and expected lift range. Advises running one test at a time with a minimum sample size calculation.
