# Content Strategy

## When to Use
- Building a content program from scratch for a startup.
- Choosing what content types to invest in first.
- Mapping content to funnel stages and buyer awareness levels.
- Identifying content pillars and topic clusters.
- Evaluating whether existing content is working and what gaps exist.
- Planning a content calendar for the next 30/60/90 days.

## Context Required
- **From startup-context**: product description, ICP, value proposition, competitive positioning, stage, existing traction, team size, available budget for content.
- **From the user**: current content assets (if any), distribution channels in use, business goals for content (traffic, leads, brand awareness, SEO), team capacity (who writes, how often), target keywords or topics of interest.

## Workflow
1. **Classify the content need** — Is the goal searchable content (SEO-driven, evergreen, pulls inbound traffic) or shareable content (social-native, timely, earns distribution through engagement)? Most startups need both but should weight one based on stage:
   - Pre-PMF: lean toward shareable (faster feedback loops, builds audience)
   - Post-PMF: lean toward searchable (compounds over time, captures demand)
2. **Identify 3-5 content pillars** — Each pillar is a broad topic area tied to the product's value proposition and ICP pain points. Use the Pillar Identification Framework:
   - What problems does your ICP search Google for?
   - What topics does your ICP discuss in communities (Slack, Reddit, Twitter)?
   - What expertise does your team uniquely hold?
   - What adjacent topics does your product naturally connect to?
3. **Map content types to pillars** — For each pillar, select from these proven content types:
   - **Use-case content**: "How to [do X] with [Product]" — bottom of funnel, high intent
   - **Hub-and-spoke**: One comprehensive pillar page with linked subtopic articles — SEO authority play
   - **Templates and tools**: Downloadable resources that solve a micro-problem — lead generation
   - **Thought leadership**: Contrarian takes, original data, founder stories — brand and trust
   - **Case studies**: Customer transformation stories — bottom of funnel, proof
   - **Comparison/alternative pages**: "[Product] vs [Competitor]" — captures switching intent
   - **Listicles and roundups**: "Top 10 tools for X" — top of funnel, shareable
4. **Score and prioritize** — Use the Content Scoring Framework (rate each piece 1-5 on each dimension):
   - **Search potential**: Is there keyword volume? Can we rank?
   - **Business alignment**: Does this attract our ICP specifically?
   - **Conversion proximity**: How close is the reader to a buying decision?
   - **Production effort**: How hard is this to create well?
   - **Differentiation**: Can we say something competitors cannot?
   - Total score determines priority. High-score items go first.
5. **Map to buyer journey** — Ensure coverage across all stages:
   - **Unaware**: Thought leadership, trend pieces, educational content
   - **Problem-aware**: Problem-focused guides, "why X matters" content
   - **Solution-aware**: Comparison pages, how-to guides, templates
   - **Product-aware**: Case studies, use-case content, demo-oriented pieces
   - **Most-aware**: Pricing pages, onboarding content, feature deep-dives
6. **Build the editorial calendar** — Assign content to a realistic publishing cadence. For early-stage startups, 1-2 high-quality pieces per week is better than daily low-effort posts.
7. **Define distribution plan** — Every piece of content needs a distribution path: SEO, email newsletter, social channels, community posting, paid promotion, or partner amplification.

## Output Format
- A content strategy document with: identified pillars, prioritized content backlog (scored), buyer journey content map, recommended publishing cadence, and distribution plan per content type.
- If building a calendar: a 30/60/90 day editorial calendar with titles, content types, target keywords, funnel stage, and assigned distribution channels.

## Frameworks & Best Practices
- **Searchable vs. Shareable**: Searchable content (SEO articles, guides) compounds over time but is slow to start. Shareable content (social posts, hot takes) gets immediate distribution but decays fast. A good strategy blends both.
- **The 80/20 split**: 80% of content should serve existing demand (what people already search for). 20% should create demand (original perspectives that build brand).
- **Topic authority over topic breadth**: It is better to own 3 topics completely than to write one article about 30 topics. Search engines and audiences reward depth.
- **Repurposing is a strategy, not an afterthought**: Every long-form piece should be planned with repurposing in mind — blog post becomes Twitter thread becomes newsletter section becomes LinkedIn carousel.
- **Content-market fit**: Just like product-market fit, content needs to resonate with a specific audience. Test topics in lightweight formats (social posts, community discussions) before investing in long-form.
- **Measure what matters**: For searchable content, track organic traffic and keyword rankings. For shareable content, track engagement and referral traffic. For conversion content, track leads and pipeline influence.
- **Distribution > production**: A great article with no distribution plan will underperform a good article with a strong distribution plan. Budget 50% of effort for distribution.

## Examples

**Example 1: New content program**
> "We're a seed-stage B2B SaaS for engineering teams. No content yet. Where do we start?"

Good output: Identifies 3-4 content pillars based on ICP pain points. Recommends starting with 2 comparison pages (capture existing demand), 3 use-case articles (bottom-funnel), and 1 thought leadership piece per month. Includes a scored backlog of 15 content ideas with priorities and a 90-day calendar. Distribution plan emphasizes LinkedIn, Hacker News, and relevant Slack communities.

**Example 2: Content audit**
> "We have 40 blog posts but they barely get traffic. What should we do?"

Good output: Asks for top-performing vs. underperforming posts, identifies content gaps using the buyer journey map, recommends which posts to update/consolidate/kill, proposes a hub-and-spoke restructuring around 3 pillar topics, and provides a 60-day optimization calendar that alternates between updating old content and publishing new gap-filling pieces.
