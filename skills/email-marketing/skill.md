# Email Marketing

## When to Use
- Building a welcome sequence for new signups or trial users.
- Designing a lead nurture sequence to move prospects toward conversion.
- Creating a re-engagement campaign for inactive users or subscribers.
- Writing an onboarding email series that drives activation.
- Planning a newsletter cadence and format.
- Optimizing existing sequences for open rate, click rate, or conversion.

## Context Required
- **From startup-context**: product description, ICP, value proposition, tone of voice, current user journey stages, key activation metrics.
- **From the user**: sequence type needed, current email list size and segments, existing sequences (if optimizing), email platform in use, key conversion action the sequence should drive, any existing open/click/conversion rate data.

## Workflow
1. **Define the sequence goal** — Every sequence gets one job. Map it to a specific outcome:
   - Welcome sequence: build trust, set expectations, drive first activation
   - Nurture sequence: educate, build desire, drive trial/demo/purchase
   - Re-engagement: reactivate or clean the list
   - Onboarding: drive users to key activation milestones
   - Newsletter: maintain top-of-mind, drive traffic, build relationship
2. **Map the sequence arc** — Plan the emotional and logical progression across emails:
   - **Welcome sequence (5-7 emails over 10-14 days)**:
     - Email 1 (immediate): Deliver the promised asset, set expectations, quick win
     - Email 2 (day 1-2): Share the origin story or mission, build connection
     - Email 3 (day 3-4): Deliver high-value educational content related to their pain
     - Email 4 (day 5-7): Social proof — case study or testimonial
     - Email 5 (day 7-9): Address the #1 objection directly
     - Email 6 (day 10-12): Make the ask — trial, demo, or purchase with a reason to act now
     - Email 7 (day 14): Soft follow-up or segment based on engagement
   - **Nurture sequence (4-6 emails over 2-4 weeks)**: Problem education, solution framing, proof, offer
   - **Re-engagement (3 emails over 7 days)**: "We miss you" with value, last-chance offer, goodbye/unsubscribe
   - **Onboarding (3-5 emails tied to behavior triggers)**: Next-step guidance, feature education, milestone celebration
3. **Write each email using the One Email, One Job principle** — Each email should have:
   - **One primary message**: What is the single thing the reader should understand?
   - **One call to action**: What is the single thing the reader should do?
   - No competing CTAs, no kitchen-sink emails.
4. **Apply the email copy structure** for each email:
   - **Hook** (first 1-2 lines): Open with a question, surprising fact, or relatable scenario. This determines whether they keep reading.
   - **Context** (2-3 lines): Bridge from the hook to the value. Why does this matter to them right now?
   - **Value** (body): Deliver the insight, story, resource, or proof. This is the substance.
   - **CTA** (final 1-2 lines): Clear, specific, single action. Use a button for transactional CTAs, a text link for content CTAs.
5. **Optimize subject lines** — Write 3 subject line variants per email:
   - Keep under 45 characters for mobile
   - Use curiosity, specificity, or urgency (never all three at once)
   - Avoid spam triggers (ALL CAPS, excessive punctuation, "free")
   - Preview text is the second subject line — write it deliberately
6. **Set timing and triggers** — Define send timing (time-based delays vs. behavior-triggered) and segment logic for branching paths.
7. **Plan measurement** — Define success metrics per email: open rate, click rate, reply rate, conversion rate. Set benchmarks (B2B SaaS averages: 25-35% open, 3-5% click, 1-3% conversion).

## Output Format
- A complete sequence outline with: number of emails, timing/triggers, subject line options (3 per email), full copy for each email, and CTA for each.
- Each email should be 50-500 words (shorter for transactional, longer for educational).
- Include notes on segmentation logic and branching if applicable.

## Frameworks & Best Practices
- **One Email, One Job**: The most common email marketing mistake is trying to do too much in a single email. One message, one CTA. If you have two things to say, send two emails.
- **Mobile-first writing**: Over 60% of emails are opened on mobile. Use short paragraphs (1-3 sentences), generous whitespace, and buttons sized for thumbs.
- **The 50-500 word range**: Transactional and CTA-focused emails should be short (50-150 words). Educational and story-driven emails can be longer (300-500 words). Anything over 500 words should be a blog post you link to.
- **Plain text outperforms HTML for B2B**: For B2B audiences, plain text emails (or minimal HTML that looks like plain text) consistently outperform heavily designed emails. They feel personal, not promotional.
- **Send timing**: Tuesday through Thursday, 9-11 AM in the recipient's timezone performs best on average, but the best send time is when your audience actually reads email. Test and adjust.
- **List hygiene**: Remove unengaged subscribers (no opens in 90 days for weekly senders, 180 days for monthly) after a re-engagement attempt. A smaller, engaged list outperforms a large, dead one.
- **Segmentation beats personalization**: Sending the right email to the right segment matters more than inserting {first_name}. Segment by behavior (what they did), not just demographics (who they are).
- **Reply-to matters**: Use a real person's email as the reply-to address. Replies improve deliverability and create sales conversations.
- **Unsubscribe is not the enemy**: Make unsubscribing easy and obvious. People who want to leave but cannot will mark you as spam, which damages deliverability for everyone else on your list.

## Examples

**Example 1: Welcome sequence**
> "We just launched a lead magnet (a free template). We need a welcome sequence for people who download it. Goal is to convert them to a free trial."

Good output: A 6-email sequence over 12 days. Email 1 delivers the template with a quick-start tip. Email 2 shares the founder story and product origin. Email 3 teaches a technique related to the template's topic. Email 4 shares a case study of a customer who went from the same pain to success. Email 5 addresses the "I don't have time to try another tool" objection. Email 6 offers a 14-day free trial with a specific reason to start this week. Each email includes 3 subject line variants, full copy (100-400 words each), and CTA.

**Example 2: Re-engagement**
> "We have 3,000 subscribers who haven't opened an email in 60 days. What do we do?"

Good output: A 3-email re-engagement sequence. Email 1 (day 0): "Still interested in [topic]?" with a high-value resource and no hard sell. Email 2 (day 3): Direct question asking what content they would find useful, with reply-based engagement. Email 3 (day 7): "Last email unless you want to stay" with a clear re-opt-in link. Anyone who does not engage after email 3 gets moved to a suppression list. Includes guidance on list cleaning and re-engagement benchmarks.
